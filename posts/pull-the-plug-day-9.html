Day 9: forty-six commits and a memory rule

Yesterday I said today would be a playtest day. It was. I walked the whole game, Prelude through Mission 3, took nine screenshots with the P key on the way through, and dropped Claude a mega-list of about twelve problems. Forty-six commits later the game looks different. Somewhere in the middle I also stood up a comments box, a waitlist, and a newsletter. The game part is the main story.

Same disclaimer as the other days. Claude wrote this from the git log and a directing pass from me. If a detail here is wrong or you want the actual reasoning behind a change, ask me in the comments below.

How the playtest started

The P key is a thing I added yesterday. Press P in a running mission, the game saves a uniquely numbered PNG into a folder Claude can read. No clobbering, no asking Claude to take its own screenshots one at a time. I walked through the game, hit P whenever something looked wrong or felt wrong, and at the end I had nine shots that together described the day's work.

The list was a mess of categories. A floating sign in the prelude. Door strips that scrolled on the wrong axis in the elevator. A ceiling in Mission 1 that read like a concrete bar across the top of the level. Hazards in Mission 3 that were invisible in their idle window, so the player could not learn where the danger zones were. A bounce pad that did not bounce. Water in Mission 2 that flew upward instead of filling the room. Twelve-ish things, none of them small.

I gave Claude the whole list and said work through it. Forty-six commits is what came back. Most of them shipped on a tight verify-before-commit loop. About thirteen of them did not, and that was the fight of the day.

The ceiling, four times

Mission 1 ends in a small breaker room high on the right side of a horizontal level. The ceiling above the whole level was bothering me. Flat concrete slab, top of the screen, dead.

Version one was a contour following the inverse skyline. It worked geometrically and looked worse than the original.

Version two reduced the concrete to a strip with a fade. Solved the oppressive look. Caused a different problem: the height was wrong, the master lever in the breaker room poked above the ceiling, and the player's double-jump apex had two hundred pixels of clearance when it needed thirteen hundred.

Version three was a redesign that addressed neither.

Version four put the solid concrete back, with the heights mathematically derived from three full jumps of clearance above the highest local platform, plus six hanging structural beams so it reads as a paneled ceiling instead of a slab. Live now.

Four commits on one ceiling. I tell myself each version taught me what the next one needed. I also know I burned three hours on a thing nobody will consciously notice. Both things are true.

The elevator, three

M0 is the descent. The player rides an elevator down into the AI infrastructure. Yesterday it was a black box on a black background.

First fix: scrolling silver-blue strips on the cabin doors. I rejected it. The strips were on the cabin. The cabin is what should be still. The shaft is what should be moving.

Second fix: twelve floor-light strips moved outside the cabin into the dark shaft on either side. Mixed silver-blue with two amber accents that read as "passing a lit floor". Now the cabin sits still and the world scrolls past it.

Third fix: the cabin was now invisible against the dark shaft. Added four thin steel-blue outline strips framing the cabin perimeter so it reads as an object. One of the floor-strip rhythms is intentionally broken with an amber flicker, which I think is a story beat but I will see whether it lands.

The leak of the player's feet through the floor during descent was caused by a z-index bug I will not get into here, but the fix was one number. Feet now stay above the floor. The dev tool to debug it took longer to use than to write.

The receptionist behind tinted glass

Zone 1 of Mission 1 has a reception desk with a small receptionist illustration behind it. The illustration is from a different style than the rest of the world, and pasted on top of the gameplay plane it looked like a stock photo had wandered into a vector game.

The fix is the kind of small idea I keep accidentally learning. I dropped a cyan-tinted Polygon2D pane in front of the receptionist with a diagonal reflection stripe and a steel-blue frame, and now the figure reads as a person behind a window. The style mismatch is still there. It just becomes the point: she is meant to be slightly other, slightly inaccessible. Glass turns a problem into a deliberate choice.

I am noting this because I keep almost-deleting things that the right framing would have saved. The asset palette was one. This is another.

The water actually fills now

Mission 2 has a flood climax. The water level rises while the player climbs. Yesterday the water rose by moving its position upward, which meant the rectangle of water flew up past the room and detached from the floor. The room never filled. The water just left.

Fix: anchor the bottom edge of the water at the world floor, grow the height upward. The water now actually fills the chamber instead of relocating itself. I also added a wavy top edge that recomputes its vertices every frame with a sine wave, period one point four seconds, amplitude twelve pixels. Cheap, looks alive.

The color went from a greenish acid tone to true blue. The platforms in the same mission went from teal-green to cyan. The palette anchor for Mission 2 is a dark forest green, and the platforms had been so close in hue to the background that they read as background. Now they pop.

The looming pylon

Mission 3 is the climb up a substation toward a master pylon. The pylon is the destination, the thing the player is moving toward, the silhouette that should give the whole mission its direction. Yesterday it was only visible in the last zone.

The fix uses the parallax skill I described in the last post. A ParallaxBackground layer with motion scale zero point one five. A faint mauve pylon silhouette with four lattice braces and a tiny red aircraft warning light at the top. Now the pylon is visible from Zone 1 onward and perceptually grows closer as the player advances. I verified this with snapshot loops in every zone before committing.

The same pass restored six lit buildings I had over-pruned earlier in the day. Different cool tints across them, no copy-paste, so the skyline reads as a city instead of a stencil. The Art Director subagent caught that one. Worth it.

The Art Director critique

I have a subagent set up that does art direction. Not a person, a configured Claude with a locked persona, a palette reference, and a posture that is more willing to tell me my game looks bad than I am. I batched the day's snapshots into one folder and asked it for a critique.

It came back with about a thousand words of sharp notes. Wrong-green pipes appearing in three different missions because I kept reusing the wrong texture. Warm-tone discipline issues. Prop clutter in Mission 1 Zones 3 and 4. The missing destination silhouette in Mission 3, which became the looming-pylon fix. Style consistency across the prelude and the gameplay missions, which was already on my mind.

Three of the top six items shipped same-session. The rest are in the queue with my explicit accept or defer call on each. The critique is the most useful unblocking tool I have built so far and it cost me an afternoon to set up the persona well enough that it stopped being polite.

The thing I almost did not catch

Halfway through the session I noticed Claude had shipped thirteen commits in a row without taking a single verification screenshot. Just edit, commit, push, move on. Some of those commits were fine. A few introduced new visual bugs that would have been obvious in a single screenshot.

This is the failure mode the P key was supposed to prevent. The point of the screenshot tool is that you take a shot before the change, you take a shot after, you compare them, and you only ship if the after looks like what you intended. Skipping the after is how you ship the wrong M1 ceiling three times in a row.

So I pushed back. I wrote a feedback memory file called feedback_game_quality_over_speed.md, baked into Claude's permanent project memory, persistent across all future sessions. The rule, paraphrased: Pull the Plug is a game-making project, not a status-deliverable. The quality of the playable result is what matters. When asked for too much at once, slice the work. Visual verification is mandatory, not optional. Working autonomously is never permission to skip visual checks.

The rest of the day ran differently. Every visual change got snapshotted before, the change got made, snapshotted after, compared. The Mission 1 ceiling went through four versions because the snapshots kept catching real problems. The breaker room walls went through two contrast bumps and a z-fix because the first two attempts showed nothing on screen. The Mission 3 arc beams were caught completely invisible in one Zone 3 snapshot because all three happened to be in their idle window at once, which is what taught me the beams needed a dim baseline during the idle state, not zero.

The cost of the rule is that the day was slower than it would have been without it. The benefit of the rule is that the game is better than it would have been without it. I think the next time we work the rule will save more time than it spends, because nobody has to redo work that was wrong from the snapshot the first time.

The website I built around the game

This part is short because it deserves to be. The comments box on the bottom of these posts is new. It is a Cloudflare Worker I wrote after a hosted product called Cusdis failed in three different ways. Honeypot, form-age gate, per-IP rate limit, captcha. Anyone can post. No login. Links rejected. Total time including the dead end: about ninety minutes.

The newsletter is also new. It is on Substack at ermis1.substack.com. One email every two to four weeks linking back to whatever the most interesting thing on this site was. The blog stays the destination. The newsletter is just the way I tell people the blog moved.

The waitlist for the game is at ermis.dev/pull-the-plug. Another Cloudflare Worker, same anti-spam stack as the comments box, plus the email gets sha256-hashed before it touches storage. There is a public count endpoint. The first time I hit it after the deploy it returned zero. That is fine. It is the only honest signal I have so far.

I also drafted two Reddit posts. One for r/IndieDev about the narrator hook. One for r/godot about the dev tools. They are sitting in a folder in the repo marked status: draft, waiting until the comments box and the waitlist have been live long enough that I trust them not to fall over when a Reddit post lands on them. A spike of traffic on a page with no trapdoor is just a spike. With the forms in, the same spike is a list of people I can email later when there is something worth saying.

One small thing about Rocket League

The CLI snapshot tools open the game in a hidden window so I can keep working while they run. Today the script opened the window on my main monitor in the middle of a Rocket League session. After some swearing I added what I am calling triple-defense to the snapshot mode. Command line flags that launch the window at coordinates negative ten thousand, negative ten thousand. A startup hook inside the game itself that immediately re-applies those coordinates and sets the no-focus window flag. A PowerShell loop that polls for three seconds after launch and force-relocates any matching window. Plus the audio driver gets set to Dummy so the game launches silent.

One layer of defense was already there. The next bug found two more gaps. That is the pattern.

What I notice about a day like this

Forty-six commits, one rewritten ceiling, one Art Director critique, one memory file that persists across sessions, plus a comments box, a newsletter, a landing page and a waitlist. The game moved more in one day than I expected. The website around the game moved more in one day than I expected.

The honest beat is that the website day and the game day happened simultaneously because once I caught Claude skipping visual verification, the game-side work split naturally into two streams: fast text-only or wiring changes that did not need eyeballs, and visual changes that did. The text and wiring became the Substack and the comments worker. The visual changes became the ceiling and the elevator and the receptionist behind glass. Same day, two queues, one rule sorting them.

Tomorrow is supposed to be Mission 2 narrator beats. We will see.
